## Introduction
In this assignment, I decided to choose to watch the movie “The Big Short”. It is a 2 hour and 10 minutes long American movie that was released in 2015, and I watched it on Apple TV. The reason that I chose this movie is that it is a very famous movie that created a huge box office and has been nominated for lots of awards. When I saw the description of this movie, I knew that this was a great opportunity for me to understand the root cause of how the 2008 financial crisis happened. I always heard that it was about money and created a huge impact on the world, like high unemployment, lots of people lost their lives, but I wanted to see how a lack of integrity and corruption actually causes the world’s economy to crash.

## Summary of the Movie
The movie basically follows a few different groups of people in the finance industry who figured out that the housing market in the US was going to collapse before anyone else did. The main characters include Dr. Michael Burry, who is a really smart but weird hedge fund manager. He spends his days looking at huge spreadsheets of thousands of individual mortgages and realizes that they are full of bad loans that people will eventually not be able to pay. He goes to the big banks and creates a way to bet against the housing market. Then there is Mark Baum, a very angry and loud fund manager who hates the corrupt banking system. He is always upset about how the banks rip off regular people. There is also Jared Vennett, a slick banker who accidentally calls Mark's team and ends up selling these bets to them. Finally, we see two younger guys, Jamie and Charlie, who start from a garage and want to get rich. They find out about this huge bubble and use a retired banker, Ben Rickert, to help them make the trades. The whole movie follows these groups as they wait for the housing market to crash. They all realized that the banks were giving out home loans to people who couldn't pay them back, and then packing these bad loans together to sell as good investments. When the housing market finally broke, these guys made a lot of money, but the movie shows how normal people lost their jobs and homes because of the banks' greed.

## Key Issues
The biggest issue in this movie is definitely the lack of integrity in the whole banking system. The banks knew they were giving loans to people with no income, no jobs, and no assets, which they called NINJA loans. But instead of being honest about how risky these loans were, the banks just mixed them up with a few good ones and paid the rating agencies, like Standard & Poor's, to give them the highest AAA rating. This is pure corruption. The rating agencies are supposed to protect investors by telling the truth about how safe an investment is. In the movie, Mark Baum goes to talk to a lady from S&P, and she literally admits they gave good ratings just so the banks wouldn't go to their competitor, Moody's. She didn't even feel bad about it. She just acted like it was normal business. Everyone just cared about making their fees right then and there, and nobody cared about the huge disaster they were building. This shows a complete failure of professional integrity because they put their own profits above the safety of the whole economy.

Another major ethical problem was how the government and the big financial institutions just turned a blind eye to the obvious fraud. When Mark Baum's team went to Florida to see the houses, they found out that mortgage brokers were getting huge bonuses for selling the worst types of adjustable-rate mortgages. These brokers were just greedy and didn't care if the people buying the houses would eventually lose them when the interest rates went up. They even laughed about targeting immigrants and poor people who didn't understand the contracts. On top of that, the big banks like Lehman Brothers and Bear Stearns were keeping all the toxic loans on their own books because they were so greedy for short-term profits. It shows a complete breakdown of professional ethics. Instead of doing what is right for the society or their clients, the bankers just created confusing products like CDOs (Collateralized Debt Obligations) just to hide the bad loans and keep making millions for themselves. Even when the loans started failing, the banks artificially kept the prices high so they could sell off their bad investments before the rest of the market found out. That is just straight-up stealing from their own clients.

## Cultural / Contextual Insights
The movie really reflects a corporate culture where greed is basically celebrated and honesty is punished. In the American financial sector during that time, it seems like making money was the only thing that mattered, and the legal rules were too weak to stop them. As Lewis (2010) mentioned in his book about the crisis, the system was designed to reward bad behavior because the people taking the risks weren't the ones who would pay the price when things went wrong. The bankers got their huge bonuses every year, and when the whole system collapsed, they didn't have to give the money back. Instead, it was the regular taxpayers who had to bail them out. This shows a huge problem in the legal and social context of the business world, where the rich and powerful can just bend the rules legally without facing any real consequences. They built a culture where if you weren't cheating the system to make maximum profit, you were seen as stupid.

This kind of corruption isn't just an American problem; we can see this happening across different cultures and societies today, including right here in Malaysia. The 1MDB (1Malaysia Development Berhad) scandal is a perfect example of this. Just like how the banks in the movie used confusing financial products to hide bad loans, the people involved in 1MDB used complex offshore accounts and fake companies to siphon billions of dollars meant for national development (Wright & Hope, 2018). Whenever there is a lot of money involved and not enough strict checking from the government, people will naturally try to find loopholes. The lesson here is that a society cannot just rely on people's personal morals when so much money is on the table. We need strong regulations and independent watchdogs that are actually independent. For example, if the rating agencies in the movie were paid by a government fund instead of the banks they were rating, they wouldn't have lied to keep their clients happy (Smith, 2018). Similarly, if there was better oversight in Malaysia, the 1MDB funds couldn't have been stolen so easily. This shows that to fight corruption, a country needs to fix the system that creates the conflict of interest in the first place. You can't just hope people in power will suddenly become good. In any culture, if the rules allow you to make millions by lying and the penalty isn't strictly enforced, people will choose to lie.

## Analysis / Personal Reflection
Watching this movie made me feel really angry and helpless at the same time. I always thought that the people running our financial systems were smart and responsible, but this movie showed that many of them were just arrogant and greedy. I really liked Mark Baum's character because he was the only one who seemed to actually care about the moral side of what was happening. He was constantly frustrated by the lies everyone was telling. There is a scene where he talks to a CDO manager in Las Vegas, and the guy is just bragging about how he creates investments out of thin air and doesn't care if people lose their homes. Mark is disgusted by it. Even though Mark made a lot of money in the end, he was depressed because he knew that the banks ruined the world and wouldn't even be punished for it. His moral dilemma of whether to sell his positions and profit from the collapse, or hold on and go down with the ship, really made me think about what I would do in his shoes. It is easy to say you want to fight the system, but when you have a chance to make billions, most people just take the money.

This movie connects deeply to real-life issues of integrity that we see every day in the news. It made me realize that corruption isn't always about someone handing over a bag of cash in a dark alley. Sometimes, it happens in bright, expensive offices by people wearing nice suits, just doing their daily jobs (Gottschalk, 2014). The people selling the bad loans probably didn't think they were evil villains; they were just following the company culture and trying to get their bonuses so they could buy a bigger house or a nicer car. This really enhanced my understanding of how important personal integrity is. It is so easy to just follow the crowd and do what everyone else is doing, especially if it is technically legal and everyone else is getting rich. But true integrity means standing up and saying no when you see something is fundamentally wrong, even if it costs you money. I learned that just because something is the industry standard doesn't mean it is right.

## Effectiveness of the Movie
I think the movie was incredibly effective in explaining such a boring and complicated topic like finance. Let's be honest, talking about mortgages and bonds is usually the perfect way to put people to sleep. But the director used a lot of creative ways to explain things. For example, they had famous actors and celebrities break the fourth wall and talk directly to the camera to explain confusing words. They used Margot Robbie in a bubble bath to explain mortgage-backed securities, and Anthony Bourdain cooking old fish to explain what a CDO is. This was a great choice because it kept me entertained while also making sure I actually understood the financial tricks the banks were using to scam people. The visual style was very fast-paced and a bit chaotic, with lots of quick cuts and shaky cameras. This really matched the crazy and out-of-control feeling of the housing bubble before it popped.

Emotionally, the movie hits really hard at the end. Throughout the film, it almost feels like a fun heist movie where the underdogs (Michael Burry, Mark Baum, and the young guys) are beating the big bad banks. You find yourself cheering for them to win. But right at the end, the movie slaps you with the reality of what their "win" actually means. Brad Pitt's character, Ben Rickert, yells at the two young guys for celebrating, reminding them that they just bet against the American economy, which means if they win, normal people lose their homes, their jobs, and their retirement savings. That scene completely flipped my feelings. The movie doesn't give you a happy ending where the bad guys go to jail. Instead, it shows the bankers getting government bailouts and paying themselves bonuses with taxpayer money. It left me with a very strong intellectual impact. The message about how dangerous greed and lack of oversight can be was extremely clear.

## Conclusion
In conclusion, "The Big Short" was a real eye-opener for me about the 2008 financial crash and the massive corruption behind it. The biggest lesson I learned is that when a system lacks transparency and accountability, unethical behavior will eventually destroy it. Going forward, I will definitely be more critical and ask more questions about things that seem too good to be true, especially when it comes to finance and investments. I won't just trust the experts blindly anymore, because the movie showed that the experts are often the ones creating the scam. I also learned that having personal integrity means doing the right thing even when the whole system is telling you to just take the money and look the other way. This movie really showed me why we need strict rules to keep greed in check, and how the actions of a few greedy bankers can ruin the lives of millions of innocent people.
